We welcome you to explore this research environment dedicated to the study of the Textual History of the Ethiopic Old Testament.
This is a research environment dedicated to the study of the Textual History of the Ethiopic Old Testament. It is a data portal, a digital humanities laboratory, a space for reports and data visualizations, with tutorials on method, and resources for dissertations. All of it is through the lens of the digital humanities – turning images into texts, texts into numbers, and numbers into stories.






New Discoveries of the Project:

  1. We have validated the working designations of EthI and EthII and given much greater precision to each as well as the relationship between the two.
  2. We have established a profile of the nature of the Period that has given us the Earliest Attested textform(s) (EthI) and the demographics of its manuscripts.
  3. We have established a profile of the nature of the Period that has given us the Standardized textform(s) (EthIIa) and the demographics of its manuscripts.
  4. We have established a profile of the nature of the Period that has given us the Modern Textus Receptus textform (EthIIb) and the demographics of its manuscripts.
  5. We have discovered a previously unidentified textform of the late 19th century which we call the LatV, a new form of the Ge’ez text modified from the Latin Vulgate. We know of this textform from three manuscripts. The books contained in these three manuscripts cover the book of the twelve, Jeremiah, Enoch, Daniel, and a few other books. We have investigated deeply only the textforms in the book of the twelve minor prophets, and in Daniel. The LatV version of Bel and the Dragon is a different recension in the LatV text.
  6. We have discovered a unique state of affairs with the textual history of Deuteronomy 32. Most of the EthI biblical manuscripts contain a version of the chapter that aligns not with the textform of biblical manuscripts, but with the version of chapter 32 known from the fifteen biblical canticles in the Psalter and chanted by the community on a regular basis.
  7. We have discovered an old, second recension of the Ethiopic Book of Job, which is more than 70% different from the textform carried in the rest of the tradition, and preserved in two manuscripts.
  8. We have discovered that the level of difference between EthI and EthII textforms of the book of Ethiopic Daniel is so great that it rises to the level of a different recension carried by EthII. This is in addition to the discovery of the LatV alternate recension of the book of Daniel.
  9. We have discovered two new recensions of the Ethiopic Book of the Song of Songs, the most copied book of the Ethiopic Old Testament (even more than the Psalms). One of these has been known before and is designated the Hebraist or Scholars edition. It contains some 72 additional strophes beyond the standard version and is known in dozens (if not hundreds) of manuscripts. But the second has been unknown and is known to us in fewer than 20 manuscripts. It contains 122 additional strophes beyond the standard version.
  10. We have discovered a form of the text of the book of Ecclesiastes that is more than 20 percent different from the rest of the tradition. We know this textform from two manuscripts in our sample, and from a third that was independently verified.
  11. We have discovered a second, previously unidentified and historically significant, recension of the book Ezekiel, translated directly from the Hebrew into Ge’ez in 1923 by noted Ethiopian scholar Kidane Wald Kifle.
  12. We have discovered a third previously unidentified recension of the LatV of Ezekiel, known to us in two manuscripts.
  13. We confirmed the existence, first noted by Schafers, of a second recension of the book of Jeremiah. We know this recension, dubbed the “Vulgar” recension, in four manuscripts.
  14. For the first time, we have analyzed the nature of the relationship between the text of the fifteen biblical canticles as transmitted in the Psalter and the form of the text carried in biblical manuscripts. We have conducted three pilot studies for the songs of Hana, Habakkuk, and Jonah. Full information about these is found on the website.
  15. We have discovered a form of the text of the book of Ruth that is more than 20 percent different from the rest of the tradition. We know this textform from two manuscripts in our sample.
